# Primary Findings: Effects of Tariffs on Small Businesses

## Direct Cost Implications

### Increased Operational Costs
- Tariffs directly increase the cost of imported goods and raw materials for small businesses
- Small businesses face the difficult choice of absorbing these costs or passing them to consumers
- Example: Tariffs imposed during the Trump administration led to significant cost increases for businesses importing goods from China
- Small businesses like WS Game Co. have faced severe financial strain due to tariffs as high as 145%, risking closure
- Foreign goods become more expensive compared to domestic alternatives, increasing operational costs for import-reliant businesses

### Impact on Profit Margins
- Small businesses often operate with thin profit margins, making them particularly vulnerable to cost increases
- Many small businesses cannot fully pass on cost increases due to competitive market pressures
- The PYMNTS survey found that while 26% of small business owners planned to increase prices due to tariffs, only 9% considered this their first line of defense

## Supply Chain Disruptions

### Supplier Instability
- Tariffs can cause suppliers to raise prices or change their terms, leading to supply chain instability
- Small businesses may struggle to find alternative suppliers or negotiate better terms
- The ongoing trade tensions between the U.S. and China have led to significant supply chain disruptions

### Inventory Management Challenges
- Tariff uncertainty forces businesses to adjust inventory practices
- Some businesses shift to "just-in-case" inventory models with 20-30% safety stock increases
- These adjustments can tie up working capital and increase storage costs

## Market Competitiveness Impacts

### Pricing Strategy Adjustments
- To maintain profit margins, small businesses often pass tariff costs on to consumers
- Price increases can lead to decreased demand if consumers perceive the prices as too high
- Domestic producers may gain a competitive advantage if their products are not subject to tariffs

### Market Share Shifts
- Import-dependent businesses may lose market share to domestic competitors
- Some businesses may be forced to exit certain product lines or markets entirely
- Regional businesses may face different competitive pressures based on local economic structures

## Sector-Specific Impacts

### Manufacturing Sector
- Small manufacturers, particularly those reliant on imported materials like steel and aluminum, have been heavily affected
- Tariffs on raw materials increase production costs, squeezing already thin profit margins
- Companies producing goods with imported steel or aluminum face higher costs, potentially leading to reduced production

### Agriculture Sector
- Small agricultural businesses, especially those exporting goods, have faced retaliatory tariffs
- Farmers exporting soybeans or other crops to countries imposing retaliatory tariffs have seen decreased sales
- Rural communities dependent on agricultural exports have experienced broader economic impacts

### Retail and Consumer Goods
- Retailers importing consumer goods face increased costs due to tariffs
- These cost increases can lead to higher prices for consumers, potentially reducing demand
- Small retailers importing electronics or clothing from countries with high tariffs struggle to maintain competitive pricing

## Regional Differences

### Border Regions
- Small businesses in regions near international borders experience more significant supply chain disruptions
- Companies relying on just-in-time delivery from Mexico face delays and increased costs
- Cross-border economic integration creates unique challenges for businesses in these regions

### Rural vs. Urban Areas
- Rural areas with a higher concentration of agricultural businesses may be more affected by tariffs on exports
- Urban areas with diverse economies might be less impacted by specific tariff measures
- Regional economic specialization creates varying degrees of tariff vulnerability

## Quantitative Economic Impact

### GDP Impact
- The introduction of tariffs in 2025 is projected to reduce U.S. real GDP growth by about 0.5 to 0.9 percentage points
- Broader economic projections suggest that tariffs could lead to significant wage reductions
- The Peterson Institute for International Economics estimates that larger tariff proposals could cost the typical American household over $2,600 annually
